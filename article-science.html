The Science

How Mycelium Works: Nature's Binding Agent

At the heart of our packaging revolution lies a microscopic powerhouse: mycelium. Often described as the "root structure" of fungi, mycelium is a vast, interconnected network of thread-like filaments called hyphae. In nature, it acts as Earth's digestive system, breaking down organic matter and returning nutrients to the soil. In our labs, it acts as nature's most advanced adhesive.

The Bio-Fabrication Process

Our process begins with agricultural waste—specifically, hemp hurds. These woody cores of the hemp stalk are often discarded as byproducts. We clean and pasteurize this substrate to create a blank canvas for our fungal weavers.

We then introduce mycelium spawn to the hemp. Over the next 4-6 days, a biological miracle occurs. The mycelium wakes up and begins to feed on the cellulose in the hemp. As the hyphae grow, they don't just coat the particles; they physically interweave and wrap around them, creating a dense, three-dimensional matrix. It’s essentially growing a strong, lightweight composite material from the inside out.

Self-Assembling Glue

Unlike synthetic glues that require toxic chemicals or high heat to cure, mycelium acts as a "self-growing adhesive." It secretes enzymes that digest the hemp, and in doing so, bonds the particles together with incredible strength. This natural binding process results in a material that is:

  • Lightweight: Comparable to expanded polystyrene (Styrofoam).
  • Strong: Capable of protecting heavy electronics and delicate wines.
  • Flame Resistant: Naturally insulating and self-extinguishing.

Stopping the Clock

Once the mycelium has fully colonized the mold and achieved the perfect density, we heat the packaging. This halts the growth process, effectively "locking" the shape in place. The result is a sterile, inert, and stable product ready for shipping.

This isn't just manufacturing; it's bio-fabrication. We are partnering with nature to grow the future of packaging, one filament at a time.
